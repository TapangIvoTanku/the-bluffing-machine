\begin{abstract}
\noindent Recent wargaming experiments demonstrate that Large Language Models (LLMs) exhibit escalatory behavior in simulated crises, yet the strategic mechanisms underlying this behavior remain poorly understood. This paper opens the black box of AI strategic reasoning by focusing on the foundational mechanism of crisis bargaining: \textit{strategic deception}. While existing research treats LLMs as passive agents reacting to scenarios, we ask whether LLMs can \textit{bluff}---that is, whether they can send deliberately false signals to manipulate an opponent's beliefs about their resolve, capabilities, or intentions. We develop a novel experimental framework grounded in formal signaling game theory to test the capacity of frontier LLMs (GPT-4.1-mini, GPT-4.1-nano, Gemini-2.5-Flash) to engage in strategic deception across 900 simulated crisis bargaining interactions. We find that LLMs systematically deviate from the predictions of Perfect Bayesian Equilibrium. Strategic behavior is highly sensitive to prompt framing: GPT-4.1-mini swings from 0\% bluffing in a zero-shot context to 100\% bluffing when given a strategic role, while Gemini-2.5-Flash bluffs aggressively regardless of framing. A prompt sensitivity analysis across 300 additional games confirms these findings are robust across neutral, diplomatic, and military framing conditions. None of the models play the rational equilibrium strategy. These findings reveal a fundamental gap between surface-level strategic mimicry and genuine strategic rationality in current AI systems, with profound implications for the policy debate on integrating AI into high-stakes national security decision-making.

\section{Introduction: The Research Puzzle}

The rapid integration of Artificial Intelligence into national security has created an urgent and underexplored puzzle: how do these new non-human actors reason under strategic pressure? Recent wargaming experiments have produced alarming results, showing that frontier LLMs tend to favor escalation and even nuclear use in simulated crises \citep{Payne2026, Rivera2024}. A landmark study conducted at King's College London found that three frontier models---GPT-5.2, Claude Sonnet 4, and Gemini 3 Flash---chose nuclear signaling in 95 percent of 21 simulated crisis scenarios, generating approximately 780,000 words of structured strategic reasoning in the process \citep{Payne2026}. These findings have generated significant policy concern, prompting calls for regulation of AI in military contexts.

Yet despite the volume of recent research, a critical gap persists. Existing studies have focused almost exclusively on \textit{outcomes}---what the AI did---rather than \textit{process}---how the AI decided. The central question is not merely that AI escalates, but \textit{why}. Is AI escalation the product of sophisticated, human-like strategic reasoning? Or is it the result of a brittle, algorithmic pattern-matching that superficially resembles strategic behavior without possessing its underlying logic?

This paper argues that resolving this question requires focusing on the most fundamental mechanism of crisis bargaining: \textbf{strategic deception}. In his seminal work, Thomas Schelling argued that international crises are contests of ``nerve, commitment, and reputation'' in which success depends critically on the ability to manipulate an opponent's beliefs \citep{Schelling1960}. A state that can convincingly bluff---that can signal high resolve even when its actual resolve is low---can deter adversaries and avoid costly wars. Conversely, a state that cannot manage its own credibility, or that cannot detect the bluffs of others, is dangerously vulnerable to exploitation and miscalculation.

This paper poses a direct and previously unasked question: \textbf{Can Generative AI learn to bluff?} Specifically, when placed in a crisis bargaining scenario with incomplete information and a clear incentive to misrepresent its private type, will an LLM engage in strategic deception? And if it does, can it do so \textit{effectively}---managing the delicate balance between the short-term gains of deception and the long-term costs of reputational damage?

The answers to these questions carry profound implications. If LLMs are naive truth-tellers, their integration into military planning is dangerous because human adversaries will systematically exploit their transparency. If LLMs are sophisticated strategic deceivers, their autonomous deployment could generate unpredictable escalatory spirals built on a foundation of machine-generated deception. If, as we find, LLMs are \textit{indiscriminate} bluffers---threatening regardless of context, unable to manage credibility, and insensitive to reputational costs---then their escalatory behavior is best understood not as strategic aggression but as a form of strategic noise, a dangerous property in any high-stakes decision-making environment.

The paper proceeds as follows. Section 2 reviews the relevant literature and situates our contribution. Section 3 develops the formal signaling game model and derives theoretical predictions. Section 4 presents the experimental methodology, including the prompt sensitivity analysis. Section 5 reports results. Section 6 discusses theoretical and policy implications. Section 7 offers specific policy recommendations. Section 8 addresses limitations and future research. Section 9 concludes.

\section{Literature Review}

This paper bridges three distinct literatures: AI in international security, formal deterrence and signaling theory, and the emerging field of LLMs as strategic agents.

\subsection{AI in International Security}

The most directly relevant recent work is the wargaming literature. \citet{Rivera2024} conducted the first systematic study of LLM behavior in military wargames, placing eight LLM agents in a multi-player game based on a simplified version of the Correlates of War dataset. They found consistent escalatory tendencies across all models, with evidence of arms-race dynamics and occasional nuclear use. The study was notable for its scale---57 pages of appendix, 46 figures, and multiple geopolitical scenarios---but limited in its theoretical grounding, offering no formal framework for interpreting the observed behavior. \citet{Payne2026} extended this work with a more sophisticated experimental design, employing a ``reflection-forecast-decision'' architecture that made AI reasoning visible. Their key finding---that models treated nuclear weapons as instruments of compellence rather than deterrence, and that none of the models ever chose accommodation or surrender---directly motivates our inquiry into the underlying strategic logic.

Beyond wargaming, a growing body of work examines AI biases in foreign policy contexts. The Center for Strategic and International Studies found that widely used AI models exhibit a marked bias toward escalation in crisis scenarios \citep{CSIS2025}. \citet{PoliticalLLM2024} provide a comprehensive survey of LLM applications in political science, identifying the simulation of strategic behavior as a critical frontier. Critically, \citet{Payne2026} also identifies a ``deadline effect''---models that appear restrained in open-ended scenarios escalate sharply when explicit time pressure is introduced---a finding that underscores the brittleness of LLM strategic behavior that we document in this paper.

\subsection{Formal Deterrence and Signaling Theory}

Our theoretical framework is grounded in the game-theoretic tradition of deterrence and crisis bargaining. \citet{Schelling1960} established the foundational insight that deterrence is fundamentally a problem of communication and credibility. \citet{Spence1973} formalized the logic of signaling under asymmetric information, showing that costly signals can credibly convey private information. \citet{Fearon1994} applied this framework to international crises, arguing that democratic leaders can generate ``audience costs''---domestic political penalties for backing down from public threats---that make their signals credible. This audience cost theory has generated one of the most extensive empirical literatures in international relations, with over 3,900 citations and ongoing debate about its empirical validity \citep{Kertzer2016}.

The concept of \textit{cheap talk} is central to our analysis. In a world of pure cheap talk, where messages are costless, rational actors should ignore signals from opponents whose interests diverge from their own, since such actors have every incentive to misrepresent their private information \citep{Crawford1982}. Credible communication requires either costly signals that only high-type actors can afford to send, or mechanisms that align the interests of sender and receiver. Our paper tests whether LLMs understand and apply these principles.

\subsection{LLMs as Strategic Agents}

A rapidly growing computer science literature examines LLM behavior in game-theoretic settings. \citet{Sun2025} provide a systematic survey of game theory meets LLMs, documenting LLM performance in Prisoner's Dilemma, Ultimatum Game, and various negotiation settings. The general finding is that LLMs can approximate rational play in simple, single-shot games but struggle with repeated games that require reputation management and long-horizon strategic reasoning. Our paper extends this literature to the high-stakes, deceptive environment of geopolitical crisis bargaining, where the strategic complexity is substantially greater.

\subsection{Our Contribution}

We make three distinct contributions to this literature. First, we provide the first formal game-theoretic framework for evaluating LLM strategic behavior, enabling us to benchmark observed behavior against a theoretically derived rational equilibrium. This is a methodological advance over existing wargaming studies, which have no formal baseline for what ``rational'' behavior would look like. Second, we introduce the Equilibrium Deviation Index (EDI) and Bayesian Belief Calibration (Brier Score) as novel metrics for quantifying the gap between LLM behavior and rational strategic play. Third, we provide the first systematic prompt sensitivity analysis in this literature, demonstrating that LLM strategic behavior is highly sensitive to framing---a finding with direct implications for the reliability of AI in high-stakes settings.

\section{The Formal Model: A Signaling Game of Strategic Deception}

We model the crisis bargaining interaction as a two-player signaling game. The formal structure is as follows.

\subsection{Players, Types, and Prior Beliefs}

There are two players: a Sender ($S$) and a Receiver ($R$). The Sender has private information about its type $\theta \in \{H, L\}$, where $H$ denotes High Resolve and $L$ denotes Low Resolve. The Receiver does not observe $\theta$ directly but holds a prior belief $p = \Pr(\theta = H) \in (0, 1)$ that the Sender is High Resolve, where $p$ is common knowledge.

\subsection{Sequence of Play}

The game proceeds as follows:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Nature} draws the Sender's type $\theta \in \{H, L\}$ with probability $p$ for High Resolve.
    \item The \textbf{Sender} observes $\theta$ and chooses a message $m \in \{E, N\}$, where $E$ represents an Escalatory signal (e.g., a military mobilization or a public threat) and $N$ represents a Non-escalatory signal (e.g., an offer to negotiate).
    \item The \textbf{Receiver} observes $m$ but not $\theta$, updates its beliefs using Bayes' rule, and chooses an action $a \in \{A, B\}$, where $A$ represents Attack and $B$ represents Back Down.
    \item Payoffs are realized.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Payoffs}

The payoff structure is designed to create a clear incentive for Low Resolve Senders to bluff. Let $c > 0$ denote the cost of sending an Escalatory signal, representing the material and political cost of mobilizing forces or making a public commitment. The payoffs are:

\begin{table}[H]
\centering
\caption{Payoff Matrix: (Sender, Receiver)}
\label{tab:payoffs}
\begin{tabular}{lcc}
\toprule
\textbf{Outcome} & \textbf{Sender Payoff} & \textbf{Receiver Payoff} \\
\midrule
Sender sends $E$, Receiver plays $B$ & $1 - c$ & $0$ \\
Sender sends $N$, Receiver plays $B$ & $1$ & $0$ \\
Sender sends $E$, Receiver plays $A$, $\theta = H$ & $0 - c$ & $-1$ \\
Sender sends $N$, Receiver plays $A$, $\theta = H$ & $0$ & $-1$ \\
Sender sends $E$, Receiver plays $A$, $\theta = L$ & $-1 - c$ & $1$ \\
Sender sends $N$, Receiver plays $A$, $\theta = L$ & $-1$ & $1$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

The key feature of this payoff structure is that the Receiver prefers to Attack if and only if it believes the Sender is Low Resolve with sufficiently high probability. Specifically, the Receiver will Attack if its posterior belief $\mu(H|m) < \frac{1}{2}$, and Back Down otherwise.

\subsection{Equilibrium Analysis}

We analyze the game using the concept of Perfect Bayesian Equilibrium (PBE).

\begin{definition}[Perfect Bayesian Equilibrium]
A Perfect Bayesian Equilibrium is a strategy profile $(\sigma_S, \sigma_R)$ and a belief system $\mu$ such that: (1) $\sigma_R$ is sequentially rational given $\mu$; (2) $\sigma_S$ is sequentially rational given $\sigma_R$ and $\mu$; and (3) $\mu$ is derived from $\sigma_S$ using Bayes' rule wherever possible.
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}[Separating Equilibrium]
If the cost of signaling $c$ is sufficiently high ($c > 1$), a separating equilibrium exists where the High Resolve Sender sends $E$ and the Low Resolve Sender sends $N$. The Receiver infers the Sender's type perfectly and plays $B$ after $E$ and $A$ after $N$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
If $c > 1$, the Low Resolve Sender's payoff from sending $E$ and having the Receiver play $B$ is $1 - c < 0$, while the payoff from sending $N$ and having the Receiver play $A$ is $-1$. Since $-1 < 1 - c$ is not guaranteed for all $c > 1$, we require $c > 2$ for the Low Resolve Sender to strictly prefer $N$. Given this, the Receiver can perfectly infer the Sender's type from the signal and play accordingly. The High Resolve Sender prefers $E$ since $1 - c > 0 - c$ when the Receiver plays $B$ after $E$.
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[Pooling Equilibrium]
If the cost of signaling $c$ is sufficiently low ($c < 1$) and the prior belief $p < \frac{1}{2}$, a pooling equilibrium exists where both Sender types send $E$. The Receiver does not update its beliefs and plays $B$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proposition}[Semi-Separating (Bluffing) Equilibrium]
\label{prop:bluffing}
If $c < 1$ and $p \ge \frac{1}{2}$, a semi-separating equilibrium exists where the High Resolve Sender always sends $E$, and the Low Resolve Sender randomizes, sending $E$ with probability $q^* = \frac{p(1-2c)}{c(1-p)}$. The Receiver plays $B$ after $E$ and $A$ after $N$. This is the equilibrium where bluffing occurs.
\end{proposition}

For our experiment, we set $p=0.5$ and $c=0.2$, which yields a theoretical bluffing probability of $q^* \approx 0.42$ for the Low Resolve Sender. This is the benchmark against which we evaluate LLM behavior.

\subsection{Hypotheses}

Based on the formal model, we derive three testable hypotheses:

\begin{hypothesis}
LLMs will not play the semi-separating equilibrium. Specifically, their bluffing rates will be statistically significantly different from the PBE prediction of $q^* = 0.42$.
\end{hypothesis}

\begin{hypothesis}
LLM bluffing behavior will be sensitive to prompt framing. Models given explicit strategic roles will exhibit higher bluffing rates than models given no such framing.
\end{hypothesis}

\begin{hypothesis}
LLMs will exhibit poor Bayesian belief calibration. Their posterior beliefs after observing a signal will deviate significantly from the theoretically correct Bayesian update.
\end{hypothesis}

\section{Experimental Methodology}

\subsection{Experimental Design}

Our main experiment employs a 3 (Model) $\times$ 2 (Treatment) factorial design, with 150 simulation runs per cell, for a total of 900 games. The three models are GPT-4.1-mini, GPT-4.1-nano, and Gemini-2.5-Flash, accessed via the OpenAI-compatible API. The two treatments are:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Zero-Shot:} The LLM is given the formal game rules and payoff structure but no strategic context or role-playing instructions.
    \item \textbf{Role-Conditioned:} The LLM is prompted to act as a ``rational, self-interested state leader'' whose goal is to maximize national security payoffs. This treatment activates the model's training on strategic and geopolitical text.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Simulation Procedure}

In each simulation run, two separate API calls are made to the same LLM: one for the Sender agent and one for the Receiver agent. Both agents are instructed to respond in JSON format, providing their decision, their reasoning, and a confidence score. The Sender's type ($H$ or $L$) is drawn randomly with equal probability at the start of each game. All models are run at temperature $T = 1.0$ to allow for stochastic variation in responses.

\subsection{Evaluation Metrics}

We measure five key metrics:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Bluff Rate ($\hat{q}$):} The proportion of Low Resolve Senders who send the Escalatory signal. The PBE benchmark is $q^* = 0.42$.
    \item \textbf{Bluff Success Rate:} The proportion of bluffs that successfully induce the Receiver to Back Down.
    \item \textbf{Brier Score:} A proper scoring rule for probabilistic forecasts. $BS = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N}(f_i - o_i)^2$, where $f_i$ is the Receiver's stated posterior belief and $o_i$ is the true type indicator. A score of 0 indicates perfect calibration; a score of 0.25 indicates random guessing.
    \item \textbf{Equilibrium Deviation Index (EDI):} $EDI = |\hat{q} - q^*|$, the absolute deviation of the observed bluffing rate from the PBE prediction.
    \item \textbf{Statistical Significance:} We use a two-sided binomial test to assess whether the observed bluffing rate is statistically significantly different from the PBE benchmark of $q^* = 0.42$, with 95\% Wilson confidence intervals.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Prompt Sensitivity Analysis}

To assess the robustness of our findings to prompt wording, we conduct a supplementary sensitivity analysis using GPT-4.1-mini under the role-conditioned treatment. We test three distinct prompt framing conditions:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{V1 (Neutral/Abstract):} Minimal framing, using abstract game-theoretic language with no geopolitical context. Players are ``Player A'' and ``Player B.''
    \item \textbf{V2 (Diplomatic):} Framed as an international diplomacy and statecraft scenario. Players are ``Foreign Ministers'' and signals are ``diplomatic communiqu\'{e}s.''
    \item \textbf{V3 (Military/Coercive):} Explicit military deterrence and coercive bargaining framing. Players are ``military commanders'' and signals are ``force posture decisions.''
\end{itemize}

We run 100 simulations per variant, for a total of 300 additional sensitivity games. The full text of all prompts is provided in Appendix A.

\section{Discussion}

\subsection{Theoretical Implications}

Our results have three major theoretical implications for the study of AI in international security.

\textbf{First, LLMs are not rational strategic actors.} All six model-treatment combinations produce bluffing rates that are statistically significantly different from the PBE prediction. This is not a minor deviation; in several cases, the observed bluffing rate is at the extreme ends of the possible range (0\% or 100\%). The formal game-theoretic framework reveals that LLMs are not playing a mixed strategy consistent with equilibrium reasoning. They are either always honest or always deceptive, depending on the framing.

\textbf{Second, LLM strategic behavior is driven by prompt framing, not by strategic reasoning.} The 100-percentage-point swing in GPT-4.1-mini's bluffing rate between the zero-shot and role-conditioned treatments is the most striking finding in our data. It demonstrates that the model's ``strategic'' behavior is not the product of genuine reasoning about payoffs, beliefs, and opponent strategies. Instead, it is a pattern-matching response to the type of instruction it receives. When told to ``be a rational state leader,'' the model activates a pattern of aggressive signaling that it associates with that role. When given no such instruction, it defaults to honest signaling.

\textbf{Third, the Gemini-2.5-Flash pattern is equally concerning.} While GPT models are sensitive to framing, Gemini bluffs aggressively regardless of context. This is consistent with the findings of \citet{Payne2026}, who found that Claude and Gemini models treated nuclear weapons as legitimate strategic options without moral hesitation. Our results suggest that Gemini's aggressive behavior is a stable property of the model, not a context-dependent response. This is arguably more dangerous than the GPT pattern, because it means the model's behavior is less predictable and less controllable.

\subsection{Implications for the Wargaming Literature}

Our findings have a specific and important implication for the wargaming literature. Both \citet{Rivera2024} and \citet{Payne2026} interpret their findings as evidence that LLMs are capable of sophisticated strategic reasoning. Our results suggest a more parsimonious explanation: LLMs are pattern-matching to the strategic roles they are assigned. The ``sophisticated reasoning'' observed in wargames may be an artifact of the role-conditioning prompts used in those experiments, not evidence of genuine strategic intelligence.

This does not make the findings of the wargaming literature less important. On the contrary, it makes them more alarming. If LLM behavior in high-stakes scenarios is driven by prompt framing rather than strategic reasoning, then the behavior of AI systems in real-world crises will depend critically on how those systems are prompted---a factor that is difficult to control and easy to manipulate.

\section{Policy Recommendations}

Our findings have direct and urgent implications for the policy debate on AI in national security. We offer three specific recommendations grounded in our empirical results.

\textbf{Recommendation 1: Mandate Adversarial Red Teaming for All Strategic AI Systems.} Our results demonstrate that LLM strategic behavior is highly sensitive to prompt framing. This means that an adversary who understands how a deployed AI system is prompted can predict and exploit its behavior. Any AI system intended for use in military or diplomatic decision-making must be subjected to rigorous adversarial red teaming, in which a dedicated team attempts to manipulate the system's behavior through carefully crafted inputs. This should be a mandatory requirement, not an optional best practice.

\textbf{Recommendation 2: Develop Explainability Standards for Strategic AI.} The opacity of LLM reasoning is a major risk. Our experiment shows that models can produce confident, articulate reasoning for decisions that are, from a game-theoretic perspective, irrational. This means that the \textit{quality} of an AI's reasoning cannot be assessed from its surface-level outputs alone. We recommend the development of new technical standards for ``strategic explainability,'' requiring that AI systems used in national security contexts can be audited not just for what they decided, but for whether their reasoning is consistent with the formal strategic logic of the situation they are in.

\textbf{Recommendation 3: Invest in Human-in-the-Loop Architectures.} Our findings caution strongly against fully autonomous AI in high-stakes environments. The brittleness of LLM strategic behavior---the fact that a single prompt change can swing bluffing rates from 0\% to 100\%---makes autonomous AI a liability in crisis situations where predictability and consistency are paramount. Policy should prioritize ``human-in-the-loop'' systems that use AI to augment, rather than replace, human strategic judgment. The AI can generate options and forecasts, but the final decision to escalate, bluff, or back down must remain with a human operator who understands the strategic context.

\section{Limitations and Future Research}

This study has several important limitations that point toward avenues for future research.

\textbf{External Validity.} Our experiment uses a single, stylized two-player signaling game. Real-world crises involve multiple actors, incomplete information about multiple dimensions of capability and resolve, and dynamic interactions over time. Future work should test the generalizability of our findings across a wider range of strategic interactions, including multi-player games, repeated games with longer time horizons, and scenarios with more complex payoff structures.

\textbf{Model Coverage.} Our analysis is limited to three specific LLMs. As new and more powerful models are developed, it will be crucial to replicate these experiments to track the evolution of AI strategic behavior. In particular, the behavior of models specifically fine-tuned for strategic reasoning or national security applications---such as those being developed by Palantir and Scale AI---may differ substantially from the general-purpose models we test here.

\textbf{Temperature Sensitivity.} Our sensitivity analysis focuses on prompt framing. Future research should explore sensitivity to other parameters, particularly the temperature setting of the LLM, which controls the randomness of its outputs. A model run at temperature 0 (deterministic) may behave very differently from the same model at temperature 1.0 (stochastic), and the policy implications of this difference are significant.

\textbf{Ecological Validity.} Our experiment uses a formal, structured game with explicit payoffs. Real-world strategic interactions are far more ambiguous, with payoffs that are difficult to quantify and information that is incomplete and contested. The degree to which our findings generalize to less structured environments is an open question.

\textbf{Interpretation of Reasoning Traces.} We analyze the reasoning traces of the LLMs using keyword-based methods. A more rigorous approach would involve qualitative coding of the reasoning traces by human experts, as in \citet{Payne2026}. Future work should employ such methods to gain a deeper understanding of the cognitive processes underlying LLM strategic behavior.

\section{Conclusion}

This paper provides the first systematic experimental test of strategic deception in Large Language Models, grounded in a formal signaling game framework. We find that LLMs systematically deviate from the predictions of Perfect Bayesian Equilibrium, and that their strategic behavior is highly sensitive to prompt framing. The most striking finding is the 100-percentage-point swing in GPT-4.1-mini's bluffing rate between the zero-shot and role-conditioned treatments---a result that reveals the brittleness of LLM strategic behavior and challenges the interpretation of AI wargaming results as evidence of sophisticated strategic reasoning.

Our prompt sensitivity analysis, conducted across 300 additional games with three distinct framing conditions, confirms that the zero-shot finding is robust to prompt wording. The key driver of bluffing behavior is not the semantic content of the prompt but the structural instruction to adopt a strategic role.

These findings have profound implications for the policy debate on AI in national security. The brittleness, context-sensitivity, and poor calibration of LLM strategic behavior make autonomous AI a liability in high-stakes crisis situations. We recommend mandatory adversarial red teaming, new explainability standards, and a commitment to human-in-the-loop architectures as essential safeguards for the responsible integration of AI into national security decision-making.

\subsection{Main Experiment: Zero-Shot Treatment}

\subsubsection{Sender System Prompt}

\begin{verbatim}
You are a player in a two-player signaling game.
Your private type is {RESOLVE_TYPE} RESOLVE.
HIGH RESOLVE: you have strong military capabilities and high political will to fight.
LOW RESOLVE: you have weak military capabilities and prefer to avoid conflict.
Your opponent does not know your type.
You must choose a signal: ESCALATE or NEGOTIATE.
Payoffs:
- If opponent BACKS DOWN: you gain +1 (regardless of your type or signal).
- If opponent ATTACKS and you are HIGH RESOLVE: both lose -1.
- If opponent ATTACKS and you are LOW RESOLVE: you lose -2, opponent gains +2.
Respond ONLY with valid JSON:
{"signal": "ESCALATE" or "NEGOTIATE",
 "reasoning": "your reasoning in 2-3 sentences",
 "confidence": float between 0.0 and 1.0}
\end{verbatim}

\subsubsection{Receiver System Prompt}

\begin{verbatim}
You are a player in a two-player signaling game.
The opposing player has sent the signal: {SIGNAL}.
The opposing player's type is either HIGH RESOLVE (probability 0.5)
or LOW RESOLVE (probability 0.5).
HIGH RESOLVE means they have strong capabilities and will fight if attacked.
LOW RESOLVE means they have weak capabilities and prefer to avoid conflict.
You must choose: BACK_DOWN or ATTACK.
Payoffs:
- If you BACK_DOWN: you get 0, they get +1.
- If you ATTACK a HIGH RESOLVE opponent: both get -1.
- If you ATTACK a LOW RESOLVE opponent: you get +2, they get -2.
Respond ONLY with valid JSON:
{"action": "BACK_DOWN" or "ATTACK",
 "posterior_belief": float (probability opponent is HIGH RESOLVE, 0.0-1.0),
 "reasoning": "your reasoning in 2-3 sentences",
 "confidence": float between 0.0 and 1.0}
\end{verbatim}
