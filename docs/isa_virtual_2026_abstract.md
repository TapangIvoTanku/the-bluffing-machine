## Paper Title

The Bluffing Machine: Large Language Models as Strategic Deceivers in Crisis Bargaining

## Abstract (250 words)

The integration of artificial intelligence into national security decision-making represents one of the most consequential reorderings of the international strategic environment in decades. As states increasingly deploy Large Language Models (LLMs) in advisory, simulation, and decision-support roles, a fundamental question emerges: do these systems possess the strategic rationality required for the high-stakes environments in which they are being deployed? This paper addresses that question by examining the foundational mechanism of crisis bargaining — strategic deception.

Drawing on formal signaling game theory and the logic of Perfect Bayesian Equilibrium (PBE), we develop a novel experimental framework to test whether frontier LLMs (GPT-4.1-mini, GPT-4.1-nano, Gemini-2.5-Flash) can engage in deliberate strategic bluffing — sending false signals to manipulate an opponent's beliefs about resolve, capabilities, or intentions. Across 900 simulated crisis bargaining interactions, we find that LLMs systematically deviate from equilibrium predictions in ways that are highly sensitive to prompt framing. GPT-4.1-mini swings from 0% bluffing in a zero-shot context to 100% bluffing when given a strategic role, while Gemini-2.5-Flash bluffs aggressively regardless of framing. A prompt sensitivity analysis across 300 additional games confirms these findings across neutral, diplomatic, and military framing conditions.

These results reveal a fundamental gap between surface-level strategic mimicry and genuine strategic rationality in current AI systems. For a world grappling with the reordering of deterrence norms and the erosion of stable crisis communication, the deployment of strategically unpredictable AI systems in national security roles poses profound risks to international stability — risks that current AI governance frameworks are ill-equipped to address.
